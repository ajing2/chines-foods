Imagine a piece of pork belly so tender it trembles at the touch of chopsticks, glazed in a mahogany sauce that's equal parts sweet, savory, and deeply aromatic. That's red braised pork — the dish that has comforted Chinese families for over a thousand years.

---

## 📜 The Poet Who Invented Comfort Food — Su Dongpo's Pork Legacy

If you think celebrity chefs are a modern invention, meet **Su Shi** (1037–1101), better known by his pen name **Su Dongpo** — one of China's greatest poets, painters, calligraphers, and, as it turns out, food writers.

In 1080, Su Dongpo was exiled to Huangzhou (modern-day Hubei province) after a political scandal. Stripped of his rank and nearly penniless, he found himself in a region where pork was absurdly cheap — "the rich won't eat it, the poor don't know how to cook it," he wrote. Ever the optimist, Su Dongpo experimented with slow-cooking pork and penned what might be history's first viral recipe, his *Ode to Pork*: "Wash the pot clean, use little water, let the wood smoke curl low and slow. Don't rush it — when the time is right, it will be beautiful on its own."

Think of it as the 11th-century equivalent of a food blog post — except written by a literary genius in exile.

When Su Dongpo was later appointed governor of Hangzhou, he brought his pork recipe with him. The grateful citizens — he had just organized the dredging of West Lake — named the dish "Dongpo Pork" (东坡肉) in his honor. Over centuries, this evolved into two distinct traditions: Dongpo Pork (large blocks, steamed until custard-soft) and Red Braised Pork (smaller cubes, wok-caramelized and simmered in soy sauce).

Today, red braised pork holds a place in Chinese culture similar to pot roast in America or boeuf bourguignon in France — it's not fancy restaurant food, it's the dish that means "home." It appears at every major holiday table, family reunion, and celebration. Chairman Mao was famously obsessed with it, claiming the fat "nourished the brain" — which gave the dish a brief political dimension during the Cultural Revolution.

Every region has its own version: Shanghai-style is sweeter and darker; Hunan-style adds chili heat; Sichuan-style incorporates doubanjiang. The version we're making today is the most beloved home-style approach — caramelized rock sugar for color, soy sauce for depth, and patient simmering for that impossibly tender texture.

## 🥘 Ingredients

**Main**
- 1.1 lb (500g) skin-on pork belly, cut into 1.5-inch (3-4cm) cubes

**For the Caramel Color (糖色)**
- 4-5 pieces rock sugar, about 20g (or 1.5 tablespoons granulated sugar)
- 1 tablespoon cooking oil

**Seasonings**
- 2 tablespoons light soy sauce (生抽)
- 1 tablespoon dark soy sauce (老抽, for color)
- 2 tablespoons Shaoxing wine (料酒, or dry sherry)
- Salt to taste (adjust after reducing sauce)

**Aromatics**
- 2 whole star anise
- 1 small cinnamon stick (about 1 inch / 3cm)
- 4-5 slices fresh ginger
- 2 sections scallion/green onion (cut into 2-inch lengths)

**Other**
- Hot water (enough to just cover the meat)

## 👨‍🍳 Instructions

### Step 1: Blanch the Pork Belly

Cut the pork belly into 1.5-inch cubes (about mahjong tile size). Place in a wok or pot with cold water, add 2 ginger slices and 1 scallion section. Bring to a boil over high heat and continue boiling for 2-3 minutes — you'll see grayish foam rising to the surface. Drain the pork and rinse each piece under warm water to remove any residual scum. Pat dry. This step removes blood and impurities, ensuring a clean, clear sauce later.

![Blanching pork belly](/images/recipes/red-braised-pork/step-blanch.jpg)

### Step 2: Caramelize the Sugar

This is the soul of red braised pork — and the step that intimidates most beginners. Heat your wok over **low heat**, add 1 tablespoon of oil, then add the rock sugar. Let it melt slowly — it will go through four stages: melting → large bubbles → small bubbles → amber color. When the sugar turns a deep reddish-amber and produces fine, dense bubbles, you're ready for the next step. The entire process takes 3-4 minutes. Never rush this — if it turns black and smokes, it's burnt and bitter. Start over.

![Caramelizing rock sugar](/images/recipes/red-braised-pork/step-caramelize-sugar.jpg)

### Step 3: Sear and Color the Pork

Immediately add the blanched pork to the caramelized sugar. Turn heat to **medium-high** and toss vigorously for 2-3 minutes until every piece is coated in the glossy caramel. Add the star anise, cinnamon, remaining ginger slices, and scallion sections. Stir-fry until fragrant (about 30 seconds). Splash the Shaoxing wine along the edge of the wok — it will sizzle and steam dramatically — then add both soy sauces and toss to combine. The pork should now be a gorgeous reddish-brown with a lacquer-like sheen.

![Searing pork in caramel](/images/recipes/red-braised-pork/step-fry.jpg)

### Step 4: Braise Low and Slow

Add enough **hot water** (never cold — cold water shocks the protein and makes the meat tough) to just cover the pork. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a **gentle simmer**. Cover with a lid and let it braise for 60-70 minutes. Resist the urge to peek — every time you lift the lid, you lose heat and extend the cooking time. The pork is ready when a chopstick slides through a piece with almost no resistance.

![Slow braising](/images/recipes/red-braised-pork/step-braise.jpg)

### Step 5: Reduce the Sauce

Remove the lid and turn the heat to **high**. Let the sauce reduce while gently turning the pork pieces to prevent sticking. As the liquid evaporates, the sauce will become thick, syrupy, and intensely glossy — it should coat each piece like varnish. This takes about 5-8 minutes. Taste and adjust salt if needed. Stop while there's still a little sauce pooling at the bottom — you'll want it for your rice.

![Reducing the sauce](/images/recipes/red-braised-pork/step-reduce-sauce.jpg)

## 💡 Pro Tips

**Rock sugar is non-negotiable**: Rock sugar (冰糖) produces a cleaner, more stable caramel with a beautiful red hue. Granulated sugar caramelizes faster and burns more easily — if you must substitute, use even lower heat and watch like a hawk.

**Always use hot water for braising**: This is perhaps the most important tip. Cold water causes the meat proteins to seize up, resulting in tough, chewy pork. Hot water keeps the collagen relaxed and allows it to break down gradually into gelatin — that's what gives red braised pork its melt-in-your-mouth quality.

**Choose the right pork belly**: Look for pieces with clearly defined alternating layers of fat and lean (3-5 layers is ideal). The fat-to-lean ratio should be roughly 40:60 or 50:50. Too lean and it'll be dry; too fatty and it'll be greasy. A good cross-section should look like a layered cake.

**Time is your secret ingredient**: 60 minutes is the minimum for tender pork. For that "trembles on the chopstick" texture, go 75-90 minutes. Beyond 2 hours, the meat may fall apart completely — delicious, but you lose the satisfying cube shape.

**Go easy on the dark soy sauce**: The color should come primarily from the caramelized sugar, not from dark soy sauce. Too much dark soy makes the dish look black rather than red, and adds a bitter edge. One tablespoon is plenty.

## 🍽 Serving Suggestions

Red braised pork is rich and intensely flavored — it needs light, fresh counterpoints to create a balanced meal:

- **Rice**: Steamed jasmine rice is the essential companion. Spooning that glossy sauce over hot rice is one of life's simple pleasures
- **Green vegetables**: Stir-fried bok choy, blanched broccoli with garlic, or a simple cucumber salad to cut through the richness
- **Soup**: A light winter melon soup or egg drop soup provides a refreshing contrast

## 🧊 Storage & Reheating

- **Refrigerate**: Keeps 3-4 days in a sealed container. A white fat layer will solidify on top — this is normal and melts away when reheated
- **Reheat**: Gently warm in a pan over low heat with a splash of water to prevent sticking. Microwave works but the texture is slightly less silky
- **Freeze**: Freezes well for up to 1 month. Red braised pork is one of those rare dishes that actually tastes *better* the next day — the sauce penetrates deeper as it sits

## ❓ FAQ

**Q: My caramel keeps burning. What am I doing wrong?**
A: Almost certainly your heat is too high. The entire caramelization must happen over low heat — it should take 3-4 minutes, not 30 seconds. Another trick: add a tablespoon of water with the sugar. The water slows down the temperature rise and gives you a wider window before it burns. Pull the trigger (add the meat) when the sugar is reddish-amber, not dark brown.

**Q: The dish is too greasy. How can I fix this?**
A: Three approaches: ① Choose pork belly with a higher lean-to-fat ratio (60:40); ② After blanching, dry-fry the pork for 2-3 minutes to render out some fat before adding sugar; ③ Refrigerate the finished dish, then skim off the solidified white fat before reheating. Note that properly braised red pork shouldn't taste greasy — the long cooking breaks down the fat into the sauce.

**Q: Can I make this in a pressure cooker or slow cooker?**
A: Yes! Pressure cooker: 25-30 minutes after reaching pressure. Slow cooker: 3-4 hours on low. However, the traditional wok method produces the best texture — the slow collagen breakdown that happens at a gentle simmer can't be perfectly replicated under pressure. If using a pressure cooker, do the sugar caramelization and searing steps in a wok first, then transfer.

**Q: Why isn't my pork red enough?**
A: The red color comes from properly caramelized sugar, not from soy sauce. If your sugar wasn't taken far enough (still pale amber rather than deep reddish-amber), the final dish will look yellowish-brown. Dark soy sauce helps but makes things look dark/black rather than red. Solution: nail the caramel stage — it should look like the color of a copper penny.

**Q: What can I add to the braise?**
A: Classic additions include: hard-boiled quail eggs (add for the last 20 minutes), potato chunks (last 20 minutes), dried tofu knots (last 15 minutes), or rehydrated dried beans. These absorb the incredible sauce and stretch the dish further.

**Q: Where can I buy rock sugar and dark soy sauce?**
A: Any Asian grocery store will carry both. Rock sugar (冰糖) comes in bags of irregular crystal chunks — look in the sugar/seasoning aisle. Dark soy sauce (老抽) is thicker and darker than regular soy sauce — Lee Kum Kee and Pearl River Bridge are reliable brands. In a pinch, you can order both on Amazon.
